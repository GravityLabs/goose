        <h1>Stress Hormones Could Predict Boxing Dominance</h1>

        <div class="entryDescription">
            <ul>
                <li class="entryAuthor">
                    By <a href="http://www.wired.com/playbook/author/bmossop/" title="Posts by Brian Mossop">Brian Mossop</a>                    <a href="mailto:brian.mossop@gmail.com">
                        <img src="http://www.wired.com/playbook/wp-content/themes/wired/images/envelope.gif" width="14" height="11" border="0" alt="Email Author" />
                    </a>
                </li>

<li id='byline-twitter' style='display:inline-block; position:relative; top:2px; padding-right: 10px;'><a href=http://www.twitter.com/bmossop><img src='/about/wp-content/gallery/global/twitter16x16.gif'></a></li>                <li class="entryDate">
                    August 18, 2010                    &nbsp;&#124;&nbsp;
                </li>
                <li class="entryTime">
                    4:16 pm                    &nbsp;&#124;&nbsp;
                </li>
                <li class="entryCategories">
                   Categories: <a href="http://www.wired.com/playbook/category/data/" title="View all posts in Data" rel="category tag">Data</a>,  <a href="http://www.wired.com/playbook/category/science/" title="View all posts in Science" rel="category tag">Science</a>                </li>

                <li class="entryEdit">

<li><iframe allowtransparency='true' frameborder='0' role='presentation' scrolling='no' src='http://platform.twitter.com/widgets/follow_button.html?screen_name=bmossop' style='width:100%;height:21px'> Follow @bmossop</iframe></li>            </ul>

        <div class="entry">
            <p><img src="http://www.wired.com/playbook/wp-content/uploads/2010/08/fight_f-660x441.jpg" alt="" title="fight_f" width="660" height="441" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-1700" /></p>
<p>On November 25, 1980, professional boxing&#8217;s two top welterweights, Sugar Ray Leonard and Roberto DurÃ¡n, squared off against each other in a rematch from five months prior, when the hard-hitting Panamanian won a 15-round, unanimous decision over the champion Leonard.</p>

<p>During the first fight, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar_Ray_Leonard">Leonard</a> unsuccessfully went toe-to-toe with <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roberto_Dur%C3%A1n">DurÃ¡n</a>, so the rematch obviously called for a change of strategy. The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_M%C3%A1s_Fight">second time around</a>, Leonard zipped around the ring, taunting Duran with both trash talk and wild punches. DurÃ¡n became increasingly frustrated, and with only a few seconds to go in the eighth round, he turned his back on Leonard, walked toward his corner, and ended the fight by uttering the now famous words to referee Octavio Meyran: <em>No mas</em>, Spanish for <em>No more</em>. DurÃ¡nâs refusal to fight stumped fans and his corner men alike. No injury occurred. No bloody gashes were to blame.  DurÃ¡n <a href="http://sports.espn.go.com/espn/espn25/story?page=moments/66">simply quit</a>.</p>
<p><object width="660" height="520"><param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/HPoWrWwwi8M?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US&amp;rel=0"></param><param name="allowFullScreen" value="true"></param><param name="allowscriptaccess" value="always"></param><embed src="http://www.youtube.com/v/HPoWrWwwi8M?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US&amp;rel=0" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" allowscriptaccess="always" allowfullscreen="true" width="660" height="520"></embed></object></p>
<p>That was nearly 30 years ago, yet scientists have uncovered evidence that may spur a re-examination of the psychological aspect of competition, so utterly embodied by DurÃ¡n&#8217;s shocking capitulation. What does it mean to be âpsyched outâ? Well, it may actually be linked to hormonal changes in the body. </p>

<p>A <a href="http://www.pnas.org/content/early/2010/06/24/1007411107.abstract">recent study</a> by Victoria Wobber and colleagues from Harvard&#8217;s Department of Human Evolutionary Biology showed that apes have rapid fluctuations in stress hormone levels right before a physical encounter, and whether the hormone increases or decreases pretty much dictates who will win &#8211; even before the competition begins.  </p>
<p><span id="more-1685"></span></p>
<p>Non-human primates in the ape superfamily are particularly well-suited for testing the physiology of competition, as these animals live in highly stratified communities, and a male quickly figures out his rank among his companions. Wobber&#8217;s study looked at the physiological changes in hormones circulating in the body in two of the closest relatives of humans, chimpanzees (<em>Pan troglodytes</em>) and bonobos (<em>Pan paniscus</em>). However, chimps and bonobos approach competition in two very different ways. Chimpanzees are highly aggressive, while bonobos get stressed out in situations where they must go head-to-head for limited food. Nonetheless, there is always a dominant male &#8211; and heâs the one that ends up with most of the food.</p>

<p>Armed with cotton swabs coated with pulverized Sweet Tarts candy, researchers took saliva samples minutes before two monkeys engaged in a staged competition for a pile of food. When dominant males won, there was a clear increase in the stress hormone right before the competition. In these cases, the dominant male was ready. He had sized up his opponent and knew, no doubt, that heâd prevail.  </p>
<p>Conversely, when the dominant male lost, the hormone level decreased before the food game. Although these apes live in very well-defined social societies &#8211; and the dominant male had more than likely battled with this opponent before and won &#8211; something had psyched him out.  </p>
<p>Human data <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/1098-2337%281981%297:3%3C215::AID-AB2480070305%3E3.0.CO;2-M/abstract">shows</a> that the level of both of these stress hormones &#8211; cortisol and testosterone &#8211; changes during competition. So while itâs hard to extrapolate the exact findings in this study to humans, chances are our hormones are also going up or down depending on whether weâre ready for competition or not.</p>

<p>Boxing fans may never know for sure why DurÃ¡n walked away from his championship fight with Leonard. Perhaps, with Sweet Tarts and a simple hormonal test, we might have seen it coming.</p>
